Restless Company

A reflective dialogue on feeling low, feeling “unmet”, and learning to sit beside inner restlessness.

This text captures a week of feeling mildly depressed, isolated, and “outdated”, followed by a slow, grounding exchange that prioritises contact over quick fixes.

Writer

This week I’ve been feeling low, mildly depressed, and outdated—stagnated, even while I move forward. I’ve felt inferior, underachieving, useless… like a carbon copy of myself.

I know I’ve accomplished a lot—education, personal projects (code, illustration, photography, writing)—but I keep wondering why I feel unfulfilled. I feel isolated, like I’m always on the outside looking in.

I think many of us avoid interacting with our own inner darkness. I write about it because I need to, and because it gives me a sense of fulfilment.

Responder

What you’re describing doesn’t sound like a lack of ability. It sounds like disconnection despite depth. The pain isn’t “I am nothing,” but closer to “I am something, but I don’t feel met.”

Being an observer—present but not fully inside the world—often accompanies people who are perceptive and internally rich. It can feel like over-functioning while under-nourished.

Machines perform; organisms need reciprocity, resonance, and being affected by others.
Responder

Tonight, we don’t need resolution. We’ll aim for contact.

1) Sitting with the feeling (without explaining it away)

Let’s name what’s present in simple language:

  • Heaviness
  • Distance
  • A sense of being unreached
  • Tiredness that isn’t physical

If you imagine this feeling sitting beside you, what is it like—quiet or restless, old or newly arrived? Does it want attention, or simply company?

2) The observer who is tired of observing

There can be a subtle grief in the role: “I see so much, yet I am not fully entered into.” Underneath the “obsolete/mechanical/copy” feeling is often longing.

  • To be affected by life, not just understand it
  • To be surprised, disrupted, altered
  • To matter relationally, not just internally

3) Gentle grounding (optional)

Notice:

  • one physical sensation
  • one sound
  • one thing you can see (without analysing)

4) A single question to hold

“What part of me has not been met yet?”

Writer

When I sit next to it, it’s restless—and there’s a sense of company. It’s hard to explain in words. It feels like an energy that uses energy all the time.

Responder

That’s precise noticing. Restless and a sense of company suggests something alive—moving, not hostile. Some experiences don’t live primarily in language; forcing them into words can exhaust them further.

Restlessness can be unspent motion—something ready to move, not toward a clear destination yet. And the sense of company can point to an inner companion that formed because external environments didn’t meet your depth.

A small, non-verbal invitation: internally tell this energy, “You don’t have to go anywhere tonight,” and observe what happens.

Restless energy that keeps you company can be creative life force without a resting place—not something to discharge, but something to house more gently.
Writer

All is fine for tonight. I might continue this tomorrow. Thank you.

Responder

You don’t need to carry the restlessness anywhere; you can let it keep you company without asking it to perform or explain itself. Even a simple closing thought like “we can pause here” can signal it’s safe to rest.
